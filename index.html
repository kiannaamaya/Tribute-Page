Katherine Johnson

One of the first Black women to work as a NASA scientist

By Kianna Hendricks | Updated Nov. 17, 2021 3:14 pm ET

Here's the timeline of the life of Katherine Johnson:

  • Katherine Johnson was born on August 26, 1918 in White Sulphur Springs, West Virginia.
  • At the age of 13, she attended the high school campus of West Virginia State College.
  • She enrolled at the historically black college of West Virginia State College at the age of 18.
  • She graduated, with the highest honors, from West Virginia State College in 1937 with a B.S. in Mathematics and French.
  • After graduating, she began teaching at a black public school in Virginia.
  • She was hired to work for NACA's Langley laboratory in 1953, which is now known as NASA.
  • She was assigned to a project in the Maneuver Loads Branch of the Flight Research Division
  • in 1957, she joined the Space Task Group program, which had a cruicial hand in the progress of space travel
  • She worked on trajectory analysis for Alan Shepherd's May 1961 mission Freedon 7, which was the United State's first human spaceflight.
  • She was also the first woman to receive credit as an author of a research report in the Flight Research Division.
  • In 1962, in preparation for the orbital mission of John Glenn, she was asked, by Glenn, to perform the same calculations as the IBM electronic calculating machine being used to control the Glenn's Frienship 7 capsule. Glenn said, if Johnson believed the numbers were good, he would be ready to take off.
  • She retired from NASA in 1986.
  • In 2015, she was awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom, the U.S.'s highest civilian honor, by President Barack Obama.
  • Katherine Johnson passed away on February 24, 2020.

"In her 33 years at NASA, Katherine was a pioneer who broke the barriers of race and gender, showing generations of young people that everyone can excel in math and science, and reach for the stars."

-- President Barack Obama, 2015
